SPIRo

Soft Pneumatic Inspection Robot for Environmental Protection.

Current industrial methods for gas leak detection, including hydrostatic testing and camera inspection, fall short of meeting todayâ€™s needs. Nearly 100 million barrels of oil are transported through pipelines annually, with 90% of the United States crude oil traveling across the country via these systems. However, pipeline leaks are both frequent and dangerous; in the U.S. alone, a significant leak occurs every 40 hours, with smaller fugitive leaks often going undetected and unreported. Inspection approaches are often inaccurate, expensive, and labor-intensive, requiring extensive human involvement. Consequently, many leaks remain unnoticed for extended periods of time, all the while causing severe damage to both the environment and populations. These leaks endanger nearby populations, cause millions of dollars in losses, and contribute to the release of nearly three million tons of methane into the atmosphere each year. Methane is uniquely concerning because it is 30 times more potent than carbon dioxide in terms of global warming potential, and it alone accounts for one-third of the worldâ€™s warming from greenhouse emissions. Therefore, early detection of gas infrastructure leaks has become an urgent and efficient way to reduce harmful emissions.

SPIRo (Soft Pipe Inspection Robot) is a pneumatically actuated soft pipe inspection robot for the accurate inspection of gas pipes in difficult-to-reach environments. It uses a compliant scissor linkage, McKibben artificial muscle actuators, and magnetic grippers. It achieves a speed of 15 mm/s when crawling on a horizontal surface and 9 mm/s when climbing up a vertical surface. It can also deform its body 60 degrees vertically and 40 degrees horizontally to clear obstacles. The robotâ€™s origami-inspired grippers support a total weight of 417 grams of extra payload to account for the necessary leak detection sensors and equipment.

Also implemented on the robot is a lightweight multimodal machine learning architecture based on CNNs and deep forest classifiers for gas pipeline leak detection systems using thermal image and gas sensor data.

I reached out to dozens of labs and professionals across the country to seek out advice for improving the robot to make it more suitable for field use. I was offered an opportunity to conduct real-field testing at the Methane Emissions Technology Evaluation Center (METEC), thoroughly evaluating SPIRoâ€™s real-world capabilities and achieving an accuracy of 95% throughout these tests. I also worked with the MIT Media Lab to implement FlowIO, a standalone air compressor system on the robot and the Southwest Research Institute for calibrating the robot in tests at home.
